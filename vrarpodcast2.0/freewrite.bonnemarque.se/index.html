Introduction

With groundbreaking technology development over the recent years, virtual reality and augmented reality have gained substantial social attention and acquired tremendous capital investment. Even though AR/VR sound novel to most people, the technology itself is actually not a new concept in human history. If we focus on the essence virtual reality and that its intention is to fulfill the viewer’s entire field of vision and making them feel present at another scene, humans have already made their earliest attempt at VR with the nineteenth century panoramic paintings. In 1960, Morton Heilig invented the first VR head mounted, entitled ‘Telesphere Mask’. This device offers stereoscopic 3D and wide vision with stereo sound. The downside is that it does not include any motion tracking. In 1987, the term ‘virtual reality’ was coined by Jaron Lanier. Through his research, Lanier developed a range of virtual reality gears include dataglove & eyephone. In 1990s, various tech companies worked on virtual reality products such as VR glasses by SEGA (1993), Nintendo Virtual Boy (1995) and the Matrix (1999). However, none of these products have proved to be successful until the 21st century during which rapid advancement has been made in the development of computer technology. The rise of smartphones of high-density display has enabled a new generation of lightweight and practical VR devices – the prices are constantly being driven down. Most recently in 2016, multiple consumer-end devices have been released by major companies, including Oculus Rift, HTC Vive, Google Day Dream and Microsoft Hololens. Market trend estimated an AR/VR market size of 215 billion U.S. Dollars by year 2021. AR VR technology is expected to become the next wave of interruptive technologies that could affect various industries and become part of our daily life. Are you ready for a new wave of immersive technologies?

Stock

When Facebook released Oculus, their stock price went down from $114 to $112. When Google released Daydream, their stock price dropped by $30 per share in only 4 days. The major conjecture behind the phenomenon is that stakeholders still consider AR/VR products as unstable and risky. Conservative investors prefer to invest in companies that focuses on its core technology rather than being spread out on too many products. The phenomenon is not just present in AR/VR industries but also quite common among many new technologies such as smart phones in the early 2000s and personal computers in the late 1980s.
